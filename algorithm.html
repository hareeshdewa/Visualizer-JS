Algorithms and Big O Notation

Algorithms are step-by-step procedures or formulas for solving problems. They can be analyzed for time and space complexity using Big O notation.


Big O notation describes the worst-case scenario of an algorithm's performance in terms of time or space complexity, allowing for easier comparison and prediction of scalability.


Various types of algorithms, such as Merge Sort, Quick Sort, Selection Sort, Insertion Sort, and Bubble Sort, have different time complexities and are analyzed using Big O notation.


Understanding the connection between algorithms and Big O notation is crucial for designing efficient algorithms and optimizing code.

Algorithm Time Complexity (Worst-case) Space Complexity Stability Definition Example Use Case
Merge Sort O(n log n) O(n) Stable Divide and conquer algorithm that divides the unsorted list into two halves, recursively sorts them, and then merges the sorted halves. To sort large datasets efficiently, such as organizing a list of customer names in alphabetical order in a database.
Quick Sort O(n^2) (worst), O(n log n) (average) O(log n) Unstable Divide and conquer algorithm that selects a pivot element and partitions the array into two sub-arrays around the pivot. When speed is crucial, such as sorting a list of exam scores to quickly identify top performers.
Selection Sort O(n^2) O(1) Unstable Simple sorting algorithm that repeatedly finds the minimum element from the unsorted sub-array and swaps it with the first element. For small datasets or when implementing a simple sorting algorithm for educational purposes.
Insertion Sort O(n^2) O(1) Stable Simple sorting algorithm that builds the final sorted array one element at a time by repeatedly shifting larger elements to the right. When dealing with nearly sorted arrays or when real-time sorting is required, such as sorting streaming data.
Bubble Sort O(n^2) O(1) Stable Simple sorting algorithm that repeatedly steps through the list, compares adjacent elements, and swaps them if they are in the wrong order. For educational purposes or when implementing a basic sorting algorithm in scenarios where efficiency is not critical.

Case Studies

Let's consider a scenario where we need to sort a list of integers in ascending order:

1. Merge Sort: Best choice for large datasets due to its O(n log n) time complexity.

2. Quick Sort: Efficient for average-sized datasets but may degrade to O(n^2) in worst-case scenarios.

3. Selection Sort: Simple but inefficient for large datasets due to its O(n^2) time complexity.

4. Insertion Sort: Suitable for small datasets or nearly sorted arrays.

5. Bubble Sort: Rarely used in practice due to its poor performance, even for small datasets.
